About This Project

In the Cornell digital archives sits a sixteenth-century broadsheet from Nuremberg. The woodcut showed spheres and crosses floating above the city at dawn, witnessed by dozens of people, recorded by a local printer who had no idea what he was looking at. He called them a sign from God. A modern reader might call them atmospheric phenomena, or propaganda, or folk hallucination. The printer called them what he could. I kept coming back to the image because it refused to sit in one category. It was astronomy and theology and journalism and art, all at once, printed on a single sheet of paper in 1561.

That refusal is what this project is about.

I have always been drawn to epistemology, to the question of how we decide what counts as knowledge and who gets to draw the borders around a discipline. The older I get, the less convinced I am that those borders hold. They are useful, certainly. Physics needs its methods; painting needs its traditions. But the most interesting work I have encountered, the work that actually changes how I see the world, tends to live in the gaps between fields.

when the categories didn't exist yet

Consider Galileo. We remember him as a scientist, but he was also a literary critic. His lectures on the architecture of Dante's Inferno, delivered to the Florentine Academy in 1588, calculated the structural engineering of Hell's ceiling. He wanted to know whether the vaulted roof over Satan's cavern could support its own weight. This was geometry in service of poetry, or poetry as a pretext for geometry. Either way, the question made no distinction between the two.

Dante himself is an even better example. The Commedia is simultaneously a work of theology, political philosophy, moral psychology, astronomy, and lyric poetry. Dante placed the Earth at the centre of nested celestial spheres because that is what Ptolemaic cosmology demanded, but he also placed it there because the architecture of salvation required a physical shape. The poem needed the universe to be a building. So he built one.

Milton did the same thing a few centuries later, constructing a cosmos for Paradise Lost that borrowed from both the old Ptolemaic model and the new Copernican one, hedging his bets in a way that would make any peer reviewer uncomfortable. His contemporary readers didn't seem to mind. They understood that a poem about the fall of humanity needed a stage, and that the stage could be assembled from whatever materials were at hand: scripture, classical myth, optical theory, political allegory.

None of these people thought they were doing interdisciplinary work. The word wouldn't have meant anything to them. They were simply thinking.

where the interesting questions live

Supernal collects stories from the places where these categories blur. Sacred cartography, celestial observation, cosmographic illustration: each of these subjects sits at an intersection. A map of Dante's Hell is a work of literary interpretation, but it is also an exercise in projective geometry. A broadsheet describing lights over Nuremberg is a piece of journalism, but it is also a theological pamphlet. The Caetani family's chromolithograph of the Commedia is a work of art, but it is also an argument about how to read a poem.

I find these intersections more honest than the neatly separated departments we have now. When I took my first philosophy course, I was struck by how much of it was really about language. When I studied art history, I kept running into mathematics. Every field, looked at closely enough, turns out to be several fields wearing a single name.

This is not an argument against specialisation. Specialists produce extraordinary work precisely because they go deep. But the assumption that depth requires walls, that you must stop thinking about aesthetics when you start thinking about physics, seems to me a relatively recent invention, and not a particularly good one.

The Renaissance thinkers I keep returning to did not have this assumption. Leonardo's notebooks move from anatomy to hydraulics to painting technique without any sense of crossing a boundary. Athanasius Kircher, the Jesuit polymath, published books on magnetism, music theory, Egyptian hieroglyphics, and volcanology, sometimes in the same volume. These people were not dilettantes. They were rigorous in ways that embarrass most modern scholarship. They simply refused to accept that rigour belonged to only one domain.

Creativity, I think, lives in the overlaps. The most compelling insights I have encountered, in school and outside of it, have come from moments when two apparently unrelated ideas turned out to share a structure. The golden ratio showing up in shell growth and temple facades. The same mathematical curves describing planetary orbits and the flight of a thrown ball. Information theory, originally about telegraph signals, becoming the foundation of modern genetics. These are not coincidences. They are evidence that the world is more connected than our filing systems suggest.

Supernal is my attempt to sit with that connectedness for a while. The articles here are not comprehensive surveys. They are readings, close and sometimes idiosyncratic, of moments when someone tried to give shape to something that resisted shaping. A mapmaker drawing the floor plan of Hell. A printer recording inexplicable lights in the sky. A university president diagramming the architecture of a poem he loved more than was probably healthy.

I don't think these stories need a moral. They are worth telling because they remind us that the boundaries between art and science, between myth and measurement, between the sacred and the empirical, have always been thinner than we pretend. The interesting questions tend to live right on top of those boundaries. They always have.

If this project has a purpose beyond the obvious one (I wanted to build a website; this is a design course), it is to suggest that the best way to understand anything is to refuse, at least for a moment, to decide what kind of thing it is.
