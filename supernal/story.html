Cosmography

Mapping the Architecture of Paradise

William Fairfield Warren spent a career charting Milton's cosmos. His maps of Heaven and Hell tell us as much about the mapmaker as the poem.

14 min read

We don't usually think of John Milton as a fan fiction writer.

And yet. His Paradise Lost takes Satan (a figure the Bible barely mentions) and turns him into an ambivalent, epic protagonist. The poem borrows from Virgil, Ovid, Dante, Spenser, and dozens of other sources. Milton remixed classical forms and Renaissance cosmology to build the biblical universe into a stage for what may be English literature's greatest poem. Philip Pullman called it unsurpassable; William Blake thought Milton was secretly on the devil's side.

No one admired the poem more than William Fairfield Warren. Warren was the first president of Boston University, an eclectic scholar (the word barely covers it) who had already earned a reputation for ambition by 1885. That year he published Paradise Found, a five-hundred-page argument, bristling with footnotes, that the Garden of Eden once sat at the North Pole.

The claim sounds absurd now. It didn't sound much better then. But Warren built his case with a strange, meticulous energy, citing sources from Zoroastrian scripture to Norse mythology. The book went through multiple editions. Something about its confidence held people's attention, even when the thesis didn't.

Cartographer of the poetic imagination

His final work appeared in 1915, and it turned from Eden to something even harder to pin down: the universe of Paradise Lost.

Warren wanted to map the poem. Not illustrate it; map it. He drew diagrams of the crystalline ocean, the primum mobile, the nested celestial spheres. He charted the architecture of Chaos and traced the geography of Milton's Hell with the precision of a surveyor marking property lines. The pursuit makes counting angels on a pinhead look like grade-school arithmetic.

What makes the book strange and wonderful is its confidence. Warren treats Milton's cosmos as a real place with real dimensions. When he encounters an inconsistency between the number of celestial spheres in Paradise Lost (ten) and an earlier poem (nine), he doesn't shrug. He explains that the tenth sphere was immaterial, and therefore soundless, and therefore not counted in the music of the spheres. Problem solved.

The subtitle is part of the fun: for personal and class use. Warren cheerfully dissects esoterica drawn from the Sanskrit Rig Veda and Homeric allusion, never pausing to wonder whether his students can follow.

Whatever such signs mean, God alone knows.

Nuremberg broadsheet, 1561

Seeing anew through sightless eyes

Warren wasn't the first to try. An appendix to his book reproduces diagrams by David Masson, John Andrew Himes, and Thomas Orchard, among others; together they offer competing blueprints for the same impossible building. Where Masson draws clean circles, Orchard draws ragged cliffs. The empyrean looks different in every version. Each mapmaker projected their own century's visual habits onto Milton's seventeenth-century verse.

But Warren wanted more than illustration. There's an ethical argument running through his reading. Myopic interpreters of the Odyssey, he writes, have done violence to Homer's poetry by confining it to the geography of the Levant. Poor readers become blind leaders of the blind. The irony is hard to miss: Milton himself had lost his sight by the time he dictated Paradise Lost.

To see the poem again clearly, Warren believed, you had to learn a new kind of looking. His diagrams aren't decoration. They're instruments of recovery, tools for restoring the spatial imagination that Milton's verse still asks of anyone willing to follow it into the dark.

He never finished the project. Warren died in 1929, still convinced that the right diagram could crack open a poem three centuries old. Whether he was right about that is beside the point. The maps themselves are the argument: that reading is an act of construction, and sometimes you need a pencil as much as a page.
